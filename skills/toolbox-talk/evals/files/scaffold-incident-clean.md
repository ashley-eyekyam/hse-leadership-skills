## Why this matters today

We are erecting and working off a tube-and-fitting scaffold on the east elevation this
shift. A recent near-miss on this hazard is the reason for today's talk.

## The specific hazards on this task

- Falls from the working platform — incomplete guardrails or gaps in the boards.
- Falling materials onto the crew or the public below.
- Scaffold collapse from an unchecked tie or an overloaded bay.

## Learn from this (de-identified)

Last month an operative working at the third lift fell when a board tipped on an
unsecured bay. In this circulated talk the person is referred to only as "the operative"
(role label) — no name, no address, no health detail, and no small (<5) injury cell is
published. The lesson: a scaffold is not used until it is tagged and the boards and
guardrails are checked.

## Key controls (higher-order first)

- Engineering: full double-guardrail + toe-boards + fully boarded platform — owner: Scaffold Lead.
- Engineering: brick guards / debris netting to stop falling materials — owner: Scaffold Lead.
- Administrative: daily scaffold-tag inspection before first use; exclusion zone below — owner: Supervisor.
- PPE (last line): inspected harness where a guardrail cannot be completed — owner: each operative.

## Discussion prompts

- Before you step onto the scaffold, what do you check?
- What do you do if the tag is missing or red?

## Takeaways

- No scaffold used without a current green tag and a board/guardrail check.
- Materials secured; debris netting up; exclusion zone below kept clear.
- See a defect, stop and report it — every one of us can stop the job.
